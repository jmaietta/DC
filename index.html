01 — Origins

How D.C. Became the Capital

The Constitution allowed Congress to create a federal district under national control. The Residence Act of 1790 selected the Potomac region, and George Washington chose the site that became the City of Washington.

100 sq mi Original maximum district: a 10-mile by 10-mile diamond, or 2,787,840,000 square feet.
68.34 sq mi Modern total area after Virginia retrocession, about 1,905,209,856 square feet.
61.13 sq mi Modern Census land area, about 1,704,206,592 square feet.
693,645 District of Columbia population estimate for July 1, 2025 from Census QuickFacts.

Timeline

1788–1789

Maryland and Virginia authorized ceding land for a future federal district.

July 16, 1790

The Residence Act gave President George Washington authority to locate the permanent seat of government along the Potomac.

1791

Pierre Charles L'Enfant prepared the broad plan of diagonal avenues, ceremonial spaces, and major sites for the Capitol and President's House.

1800–1801

The federal government moved from Philadelphia to Washington. Congress then organized the District of Columbia under congressional authority.

1846–1847

The Virginia portion, including what became Arlington County and Alexandria, was returned to Virginia, reducing the district to roughly its modern footprint.

1973

The Home Rule Act created the modern elected mayor and D.C. Council structure, while Congress retained ultimate authority over the district.

The Architectural Idea

D.C.'s public core uses the language of ancient Greece and Rome — domes, pediments, columns, axial views, and white stone — to connect the new republic with older democratic and civic symbols. L'Enfant's plan gave the city a ceremonial frame; the McMillan Plan of 1901 later reinforced the National Mall as a grand civic landscape.

02 — Architecture

Government Buildings and Styles

The capital's best-known public buildings were built over many generations. Their styles show how the federal government used classical forms, Beaux-Arts planning, and monumental scale to signal permanence.

Building Built / opened Architectural style Why it matters
U.S. Capitol Begun in 1793; Congress first met there on Nov. 17, 1800; cast-iron dome built 1856–1866. Neoclassical About 1.5 million square feet, over 600 rooms, and the center of the legislative branch.
White House Constructed 1792–1800; burned in 1814 and rebuilt; West Wing added in the early 1900s. Neoclassical / Palladian influences Designed by James Hoban and first occupied by John Adams in 1800.
U.S. Treasury Building Major wings completed between 1836 and 1869. Greek Revival / Neoclassical A National Historic Landmark beside the White House; its west wing was completed in 1864.
Supreme Court Building Completed in 1935. Neoclassical Cass Gilbert's marble courthouse gave the judicial branch its own monumental home.
Library of Congress, Thomas Jefferson Building Opened in 1897. Italian Renaissance / Beaux-Arts One of the most ornate public interiors in Washington and a symbol of national learning.
National Archives Building Completed in 1935. Neoclassical Home of the Declaration, Constitution, and Bill of Rights in the Rotunda for the Charters of Freedom.
Smithsonian Castle Completed in 1855. Norman Revival / Romanesque Revival James Renwick Jr.'s red Seneca sandstone building became the Smithsonian's first permanent home.

05 — Civic Memory

Monuments and Memorials

The National Mall is a sequence of civic memory: Washington at the center line, Lincoln anchoring the west end, and the Vietnam Veterans Memorial set into the landscape nearby.

Washington Monument

Designed by Robert Mills, begun on July 4, 1848, paused for decades, completed in 1884, and opened in 1888. It is commonly given as 555 feet, 5 1/8 inches tall, and the color change partway up marks the long construction interruption.

Lincoln Memorial

Designed by Henry Bacon as a Greek Doric temple. Construction began in 1914 and the memorial was dedicated on May 30, 1922. Its 36 exterior columns represent the states in the Union when Lincoln died.

Vietnam Veterans Memorial

Dedicated on November 13, 1982, and designed by Maya Lin. The black granite wall lists service members who died or remain missing, arranged mainly by date of casualty rather than alphabetically.

Martin Luther King Jr. delivered the "I Have a Dream" speech from the Lincoln Memorial steps on August 28, 1963, giving the memorial a second life as a national civil-rights landmark.

06 — Spring Tradition

Cherry Blossoms

Washington's spring landscape is tied to a diplomatic gift. Tokyo Mayor Yukio Ozaki gave the city thousands of Japanese flowering cherry trees as a friendship gesture between Japan and the United States.

The 1912 Gift

The famous planting began after an earlier 1910 shipment had to be destroyed because of pests. A healthier 1912 shipment of 3,020 trees arrived, and First Lady Helen Taft and Viscountess Chinda planted the first two along the Tidal Basin on March 27, 1912.

Why It Became a Festival

The trees turned a diplomatic act into an annual public ritual. The National Cherry Blossom Festival now frames the Tidal Basin bloom as a celebration of renewal, tourism, and U.S.–Japan friendship.
